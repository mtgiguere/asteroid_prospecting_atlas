# Glossary

This glossary explains astronomical terms used in the Asteroid Prospecting Atlas.

## Asteroid

A small rocky body that orbits the Sun.  
Most asteroids are located in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.

---

## Semi-Major Axis (a)

The average distance between an asteroid and the Sun.

It represents the size of the asteroid's orbit.

Measured in **astronomical units (AU)**.

1 AU ≈ distance between Earth and the Sun.

---

## Eccentricity (e)

A measure of how stretched an orbit is.

• e = 0 → perfectly circular orbit  
• e → 1 → very elongated orbit

---

## Inclination

The tilt of an asteroid's orbit relative to Earth's orbital plane.

Measured in degrees.

---

## Perihelion

The closest point of an orbit to the Sun.

Formula:

```
perihelion = a(1 − e)
```

---

## Aphelion

The farthest point of an orbit from the Sun.

Formula:

```
aphelion = a(1 + e)
```

---

## Earth-Crossing Asteroid

An asteroid whose orbit intersects the distance of Earth's orbit around the Sun.

These asteroids are important for both planetary defense and mission planning.

---

## Accessibility Score

A heuristic value estimating how similar an asteroid's orbit is to Earth's.

Lower scores indicate orbits that are easier to reach from Earth.

---

## Prospecting Score

A composite score combining orbital accessibility with physical characteristics (estimated diameter, albedo, absolute magnitude) to rank asteroids by their potential value as mining or exploration targets.

Higher scores indicate more promising candidates.

---

## Longitude of Ascending Node (Ω)

The angle in the ecliptic plane between a reference direction and the point where an asteroid's orbit crosses the ecliptic from south to north.

Together with the argument of periapsis, it defines the orientation of the orbit in 3D space.

---

## Argument of Periapsis (ω)

The angle between the ascending node and the perihelion point, measured in the plane of the orbit.

It determines which direction in the orbit the closest approach to the Sun occurs.
